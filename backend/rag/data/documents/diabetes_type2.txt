TITLE: Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet / American Diabetes Association
URL: https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/diabetes

OVERVIEW
Type 2 diabetes is a chronic metabolic disease characterized by elevated blood glucose levels (hyperglycemia) resulting from the body's ineffective use of insulin. It accounts for approximately 90-95% of all diabetes cases worldwide and is largely preventable through lifestyle modifications.

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY
In type 2 diabetes:
1. Insulin resistance: Body cells don't respond properly to insulin
2. Relative insulin deficiency: Pancreas cannot produce enough insulin to overcome resistance
3. Progressive beta-cell dysfunction: Insulin-producing cells gradually fail

RISK FACTORS
Non-modifiable:
- Age (>45 years)
- Family history of diabetes
- Ethnicity (higher risk in certain populations)
- History of gestational diabetes

Modifiable:
- Overweight or obesity (BMI >25)
- Physical inactivity
- Unhealthy diet
- Hypertension
- Abnormal cholesterol levels
- Polycystic ovary syndrome

SYMPTOMS
Type 2 diabetes often develops gradually. Symptoms include:
- Increased thirst (polydipsia)
- Frequent urination (polyuria)
- Increased hunger (polyphagia)
- Unintended weight loss
- Fatigue
- Blurred vision
- Slow-healing sores
- Frequent infections
- Numbness or tingling in hands/feet
- Areas of darkened skin (acanthosis nigricans)

Many people remain asymptomatic for years, making screening essential.

DIAGNOSIS
Diagnostic criteria (any of the following):
- Fasting plasma glucose ≥126 mg/dL (7.0 mmol/L)
- 2-hour plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL during oral glucose tolerance test
- HbA1c ≥6.5%
- Random plasma glucose ≥200 mg/dL with classic symptoms

COMPLICATIONS
Chronic hyperglycemia leads to:
- Cardiovascular disease (heart attack, stroke)
- Nephropathy (kidney disease)
- Retinopathy (vision loss)
- Neuropathy (nerve damage)
- Foot problems potentially leading to amputation
- Skin conditions
- Hearing impairment
- Alzheimer's disease association
- Depression

TREATMENT
Lifestyle modifications:
- Healthy eating patterns
- Regular physical activity (150 min/week moderate exercise)
- Weight management (5-10% loss significantly improves outcomes)
- Smoking cessation

Medications:
- Metformin (first-line therapy)
- Sulfonylureas
- DPP-4 inhibitors
- GLP-1 receptor agonists
- SGLT2 inhibitors
- Thiazolidinediones
- Insulin (when other treatments insufficient)

Monitoring:
- Regular blood glucose self-monitoring
- HbA1c testing every 3-6 months
- Annual comprehensive exams (eyes, feet, kidneys)

PREVENTION
Type 2 diabetes can often be prevented or delayed through:
- Maintaining healthy weight
- Regular physical activity
- Balanced diet rich in fiber, low in processed foods
- Avoiding tobacco use
- Regular health screenings

Studies show lifestyle interventions can reduce diabetes risk by 58% in high-risk individuals.

PROGNOSIS
With proper management, people with type 2 diabetes can live long, healthy lives. Key factors for good outcomes include:
- Maintaining near-normal blood glucose levels
- Blood pressure control
- Cholesterol management
- Regular medical care
- Patient education and self-management
