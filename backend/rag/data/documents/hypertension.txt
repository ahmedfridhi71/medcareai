TITLE: Hypertension (High Blood Pressure)
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet / American Heart Association
URL: https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/hypertension

OVERVIEW
Hypertension, or high blood pressure, is a chronic medical condition in which blood pressure in the arteries is persistently elevated. It affects over 1.28 billion adults worldwide and is a major risk factor for cardiovascular disease, stroke, and kidney disease. Often called the "silent killer," hypertension frequently has no symptoms until serious complications develop.

BLOOD PRESSURE CLASSIFICATION
Blood pressure is measured in millimeters of mercury (mmHg):
- Normal: <120/<80 mmHg
- Elevated: 120-129/<80 mmHg
- Stage 1 Hypertension: 130-139/80-89 mmHg
- Stage 2 Hypertension: ≥140/≥90 mmHg
- Hypertensive Crisis: >180/>120 mmHg

TYPES
Primary (Essential) Hypertension:
- Most common (90-95% of cases)
- No identifiable cause
- Develops gradually over years
- Associated with genetic and lifestyle factors

Secondary Hypertension:
- Caused by underlying condition
- Tends to appear suddenly
- Causes include kidney disease, adrenal gland tumors, thyroid problems, certain medications, obstructive sleep apnea

RISK FACTORS
Non-modifiable:
- Age (risk increases with age)
- Family history
- Race (higher prevalence in African ancestry)

Modifiable:
- Obesity or overweight
- Physical inactivity
- High sodium diet
- Low potassium intake
- Excessive alcohol consumption
- Tobacco use
- Chronic stress
- Chronic conditions (diabetes, kidney disease)

SYMPTOMS
Most people with hypertension experience no symptoms. When symptoms occur, they may include:
- Headaches (especially in morning)
- Dizziness
- Shortness of breath
- Nosebleeds
- Chest pain
- Visual changes
- Blood in urine

Severe hypertension can cause:
- Severe headache
- Confusion
- Nausea and vomiting
- Chest pain
- Vision problems

COMPLICATIONS
Uncontrolled hypertension damages blood vessels and organs:
- Heart disease (heart failure, coronary artery disease)
- Stroke
- Kidney disease and failure
- Vision loss (retinopathy)
- Sexual dysfunction
- Peripheral artery disease
- Aneurysm
- Cognitive impairment

DIAGNOSIS
- Multiple blood pressure readings on separate occasions
- 24-hour ambulatory blood pressure monitoring
- Home blood pressure monitoring
- Laboratory tests (blood, urine)
- ECG, echocardiogram
- Assessment for secondary causes

TREATMENT
Lifestyle Modifications:
- DASH diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension)
- Sodium restriction (<2,300 mg/day, ideally <1,500 mg)
- Regular aerobic exercise (150 min/week)
- Weight loss if overweight
- Limit alcohol consumption
- Quit smoking
- Stress management

Medications (when lifestyle changes insufficient):
- Diuretics (thiazides are first-line)
- ACE inhibitors
- Angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARBs)
- Calcium channel blockers
- Beta-blockers
- Often requires combination therapy

MONITORING
- Regular blood pressure checks
- Home monitoring recommended
- Annual evaluation of end-organ damage
- Medication compliance monitoring

PREVENTION
- Maintain healthy weight
- Exercise regularly
- Eat balanced diet low in sodium
- Limit alcohol
- Don't smoke
- Manage stress
- Regular health screenings

WHEN TO SEEK IMMEDIATE CARE
Seek emergency care if blood pressure exceeds 180/120 mmHg with:
- Severe headache
- Chest pain
- Shortness of breath
- Numbness or weakness
- Vision changes
- Difficulty speaking
