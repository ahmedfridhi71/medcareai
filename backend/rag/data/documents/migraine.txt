TITLE: Migraine
SOURCE: WHO Fact Sheet / American Migraine Foundation
URL: https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/headache-disorders

OVERVIEW
Migraine is a neurological disease characterized by recurrent episodes of moderate to severe headache, often accompanied by nausea, vomiting, and sensitivity to light and sound. It affects approximately 12% of the global population and is three times more common in women than men. Migraine is the second leading cause of disability worldwide.

TYPES OF MIGRAINE
Migraine without aura (common migraine):
- 70-75% of migraine cases
- No warning signs before headache

Migraine with aura:
- 25-30% of cases
- Visual, sensory, or speech disturbances precede headache
- Aura typically lasts 20-60 minutes

Other types:
- Chronic migraine (≥15 headache days/month)
- Hemiplegic migraine (temporary paralysis)
- Vestibular migraine (vertigo symptoms)
- Menstrual migraine
- Silent migraine (aura without headache)

PHASES OF MIGRAINE
1. Prodrome (hours to days before):
   - Mood changes
   - Food cravings
   - Neck stiffness
   - Increased urination
   - Yawning

2. Aura (if present):
   - Visual disturbances (flashes, blind spots)
   - Tingling sensations
   - Speech difficulties
   - Weakness

3. Headache:
   - Typically unilateral, pulsating
   - Moderate to severe intensity
   - Worsened by physical activity
   - Duration: 4-72 hours

4. Postdrome:
   - Fatigue
   - Confusion
   - "Hangover" feeling
   - Mood changes

SYMPTOMS
During headache phase:
- Throbbing or pulsating head pain
- Pain on one or both sides
- Nausea and vomiting
- Photophobia (light sensitivity)
- Phonophobia (sound sensitivity)
- Osmophobia (smell sensitivity)
- Blurred vision
- Lightheadedness

TRIGGERS
Common migraine triggers include:
- Hormonal changes (menstruation, pregnancy)
- Stress or stress letdown
- Sleep disturbances
- Weather changes
- Certain foods (aged cheese, alcohol, caffeine)
- Food additives (MSG, nitrates)
- Sensory stimuli (bright lights, loud sounds, strong smells)
- Physical exertion
- Medications (vasodilators, oral contraceptives)
- Dehydration
- Skipped meals

DIAGNOSIS
Based on clinical history:
- At least 5 attacks meeting criteria
- Headache lasting 4-72 hours
- At least 2 of: unilateral, pulsating, moderate/severe, aggravated by activity
- At least 1 of: nausea/vomiting, photophobia/phonophobia
- Not attributed to another disorder

Testing (to rule out other causes):
- Neurological examination
- MRI or CT scan if indicated
- Blood tests

TREATMENT
Acute Treatment:
- NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen)
- Triptans (sumatriptan, rizatriptan)
- Ergotamines
- Gepants (CGRP antagonists)
- Anti-nausea medications
- Ditans

Preventive Treatment (for frequent migraines):
- Beta-blockers (propranolol)
- Antidepressants (amitriptyline)
- Antiseizure drugs (topiramate, valproate)
- CGRP monoclonal antibodies (erenumab, fremanezumab)
- Botox injections
- Blood pressure medications

Non-pharmacological:
- Biofeedback
- Cognitive behavioral therapy
- Acupuncture
- Relaxation techniques
- Regular sleep schedule
- Exercise

PREVENTION
- Identify and avoid triggers
- Maintain regular sleep patterns
- Stay hydrated
- Exercise regularly
- Manage stress
- Keep a headache diary
- Consider dietary modifications

WHEN TO SEEK MEDICAL CARE
See a doctor if:
- Headache pattern changes
- Headaches become more severe or frequent
- New type of headache
- Over-the-counter medications don't help

Seek emergency care for:
- Sudden, severe headache ("thunderclap")
- Headache with fever, stiff neck, rash
- Headache after head injury
- Headache with confusion or seizures
- Double vision or vision loss
- Weakness or numbness
